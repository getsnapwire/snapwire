Idea Review: Snapwire — "The Real-Time Circuit Breaker for AI Agents"
Your idea for Snapwire is incredibly well-timed for the 2026 landscape. We have officially moved past "Model Safety" (preventing an AI from saying something mean) and into "Operational Safety" (preventing an AI from bankrupting a company or deleting a database).

Here is a breakdown of where this falls in the market, its potential, and a critical review of your feature set.

1. Market Placement: The "Operational Resilience" Layer
Snapwire falls squarely into the AI Gateway / Proxy category, specifically serving as a Control Plane for Agentic AI.

The Gap You Fill: Large enterprises are currently struggling with "Shadow AI"—teams deploying autonomous agents (AutoGPT, CrewAI) without IT oversight.

The "Pick & Shovel": You aren't building the agent; you are building the "OS Kernel" that decides if the agent's request is allowed to reach the hardware (the APIs).

2. High-Potential Features (The "Killers")
Fuse Breaker (Loop Detector): This is your most marketable "day one" feature. In 2026, "Agent Thrashing" (AI getting stuck in a tool-call loop) is a major hidden cost for businesses. Quantifying the "estimated savings" on the dashboard is a brilliant psychological hook for CFOs.

Snap-Tokens: This is a top-tier security pattern. Treating agents as First-Class Identities with revocable, scoped tokens—rather than letting them pass raw secret keys—is the only way enterprise security will allow autonomous agents to touch production systems.

Snap-Card Review Queue: The "Reject, Edit & Release" workflow is exactly the "Human-in-the-loop" model required by the EU AI Act of 2026. It allows for "Degraded Operations" where the agent isn't killed, but simply paused for a manual logic check.

3. Critical Review & Feedback
The Strengths:
Deterministic vs. Heuristic Balance: You’ve correctly identified that security people hate "Black Box" security. By making the core features deterministic (regex, JSON schema, loop counts) and labeling the LLM features as [HEURISTIC], you gain the trust of SysAdmins.

Open Source Strategy: Using Apache 2.0 and building a pluggable /rules/ directory creates a "Community Immune System." As new "Agent Jailbreaks" are discovered, the community can push a Python rule file to your repo, making Snapwire more valuable every day.

The Risks/Weaknesses:
Performance Latency: Intercepting every tool call with a Python/Flask gateway can introduce "inference lag." In 2026, high-frequency agents need sub-50ms overhead.

Recommendation: Consider moving the core "Fuse Breaker" logic to a Rust or Go sidecar if you scale, while keeping the dashboard in Python.

The "Write-Access" Paradox: If your Identity Vault stores the credentials, Snapwire itself becomes the single biggest target for hackers. * Recommendation: Ensure you have a clear plan for Hardware Security Module (HSM) integration (like AWS KMS or HashiCorp Vault) rather than just a local Postgres table.

There are competitors, but your specific "Circuit Breaker" branding is unique:

AgentGateway (Rust): Very fast, focuses on Kubernetes. More "infrastructure" than "dashboard."

Guardrails AI: Focuses more on data quality (making sure the AI doesn't lie) rather than system safety (making sure the AI doesn't loop or exfiltrate).

CyberArk/Okta: They handle the identity, but they don't have the "Loop Detection" or "Tool Edit" UI that a developer needs.

Snapwire isn't just a "security tool"—it's a Cost & Compliance Dashboard. In 2026, companies won't stop using AI because it's "dangerous"; they'll stop because it's "unpredictable and expensive." You solve both.
