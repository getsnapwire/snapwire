In 2026, the legal landscape for AI is defined by **"Duty of Care."** Your current messaging is strategically brilliant because it positions Snapwire as a **mechanical utility** (a "fuse") rather than a **legal agent** (a "manager").

However, to ensure you are protected during an acquisition—where lawyers will pick apart every word—there are three specific areas where we should tighten the language to avoid "Feature Liability."

---

### **1. The "Deterministic" Shield**

Your current use of the word **"Deterministic"** is your best legal defense.

* **The Risk:** If you claim your AI "understands" safety, you are liable when the AI hallucinates and fails to block a bad action.
* **The Fix:** Always frame the core protection as a "Hard Fuse."
* **Change:** Instead of saying "Snapwire ensures your agent is safe," say **"Snapwire enforces user-defined deterministic limits."** * **Why:** This shifts the liability to the **User** (who defined the limits) rather than your **Software** (which just enforced them).

### **2. The "Deception Audit" Disclaimer**

You mentioned "Goal Drift" and "Deception Audits" in your roadmap/features. These are "High Liability" terms.

* **The Risk:** If an agent deceptions a user and Snapwire didn't catch it, the user could claim "Failure of Promised Function."
* **The Fix:** Add the word **"Probabilistic"** or **"Heuristic"** to these specific features.
* **Homepage Edit:** *"Goal Drift Alerts: Uses **probabilistic** modeling to flag behavioral shifts for human review."*

### **3. The "Human-in-the-Loop" Mandate**

To avoid being blamed for a "False Negative" (letting a bad action through), your site must emphasize that you are a **Decision-Support Tool**, not a Decision-Maker.
